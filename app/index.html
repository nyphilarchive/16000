16,000: A CONCERT OF RECORD

January 7, 2016 marks the 16,000th concert of the New York Philharmonic, a record unmatched by any other symphony orchestra in the world.

As the archives reveal, the demands of such an extraordinary soloist were high. Rachmaninoff’s managers negotiated unheardof percentage fees for his appearances, and lucrative royalties for his compositions. He insisted never to be heard on the radio, so the Philharmonic prepared different programs for their Sunday broadcast concerts during his visiting weeks. Tellingly, Rachmaninoff would appear mostly with guest conductors and the more accommodating music directors such as Josef Stransky and Walter Damrosch. The Philharmonic’s “star” personalities—Willem Mengelberg, Bruno Walter, and Gustav Mahler—collaborated with him only once; Arturo Toscanini and Artur Rodzinski did not engage him at all.

When the Philharmonic did play with Rachmaninoff, however, it was greeted by capacity audiences and attention given only to the most famous artists of the day. His closest rivals were Josef Hoffman, Vladimir Horowitz, and Fritz Kreisler, but Rachmaninoff exceeded their fame (and pay) as a soloist with his own compositions to showcase. As Olin Downes, music critic for The New York Times wrote in an article after the composer’s passing, “Rachmaninoff was never anything but impregnably himself, and in himself was greatness.”

  • 1ST Concert

    December 7th, 1842

    The New York Philharmonic performed its first concert to an audience of 700 at the Apollo Rooms, a multi-purpose hall on Broadway below Canal Street. The first piece they performed was Beethoven’s Fifth, its second performance in the U.S.

  • 50TH Concert

    January 20th, 1855

    Twelve years after the Philharmonic’s founding, the 50th concert was performed at Niblo’s Garden at Broadway and Prince Street, the Philharmonic’s second home and the best stage in the City. The house could seat 3,000—more than four times that of the Apollo Rooms.

  • 100TH Concert

    November 4th, 1865

    At the time of the 100th concert there was no assigned seating for ticket holders. The rowdy crowd depicted below is a rendition of the audience rushing the doors of the Academy of Music, the Philharmonic’s home, to get the best seats.

  • 300TH Concert

    January 10th, 1896

    At the time of the 300th concert New York had passed 1.5 million in population and was rapidly expanding. The Philharmonic had moved to Carnegie Hall in 1892 and was under the direction of their first visionary music director, Wagner disciple Anton Seidl.

  • 500TH Concert

    March 14th, 1908

    Wassily Safonoff, renowned Russian conductor led the Philharmonic’s 500th concert. Two months before Gustav Mahler had made his New York debut at the Met; Philharmonic directors would soon begin talks with Mahler that would lead to his music directorship in 1909.

  • 1000TH Concert

    March 5th, 1916

    Impending war with Germany spurred a heated debate over the appropriateness of German music on Philharmonic programs, resulting in an upswing in music from allied countries. This program featured music by Liszt and Tchaikovsky, with no Germans represented.

  • 5000TH Concert

    December 13th, 1951

    George Szell frequent guest at the Philharmonic conducted the 5,000th concert which included Kalliwoda’s Overture, featured on the Philharmonic’s first concert. After the Star Spangled Banner Mayor Impellitteri presented a plaque to the Orchestra.

  • 6000TH Concert

    September 9th, 1959

    The Philharmonic performed its 6,000th concert in Moscow, on a tour to Europe and the Near East with a strong peace-keeping mission. While conductor Thomas Schippers led this concert, Music Director Leonard Bernstein led the tour, strategically programming American and Russian music that highlighted the similarities between the two cultures.

  • 8000TH Concert

    April 17th, 1971

    Then Music Director Pierre Boulez conducted the 8,000 concert featuring Second Viennese School composers. Boulez worked hard to push the boundaries of the Philharmonic’s repertoire to include serial and contemporary music, starting the Philharmonic’s first season-long new music series, Prospective Encounters.

  • 10000TH Concert

    March 7th, 1982

    The biggest celebration for a numbered concert came with Zubin Mehta’s performance of Mahler’s Symphony No. 2 Resurrection. 300 composers, conductors and soloists attended; President Regan wrote “we are proud that a country as young as ours may lay claim to such an exciting musical accomplishment.”

  • 12000TH Concert

    April 7th, 1993

    Then Music Director Kurt Masur celebrated 12,000 with the Philharmonic on tour in his home town of Leipzig. Appropriately programmed was Dvorak’s New World Symphony, as the Philharmonic had given the world premiere of this piece in 1893 while the composer was living in New York.
